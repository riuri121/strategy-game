# Session Minutes: Sol Corp Economic System Design
**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Participants:** Dan (Isdanbull, Dardobul)  
**Topic:** High-level mechanics and design direction for space industrialization game

---

## Core Concept

**Game Premise:** Player controls a spaceborne corporation competing to industrialize the solar system while interacting with Earth factions.

- **Setting:** Lunar mines, Mars colonies, Earth orbit, plus four Earth-based factions (US, Europe, India, China)
- **Primary loop:** Extract/produce goods in space → export to Earth → reinvest profits → expand operations
- **Competition:** Multiple player corporations competing for market dominance

---

## Earth Factions & Dynamic Equilibrium

Four Earth factions exist in a **stable but dynamic equilibrium:**
- At any given time, factions have different political states (e.g., "US-China at war → military demand up; India at peace → consumer goods demand up")
- No faction permanently dominates or collapses
- Shifts are driven by events, downturns, and strategic shifts—not player-determined
- Corporations respond to demand patterns by adjusting production/export

---

## Market System (Victoria 3-Inspired)

Each location has its **own market**:
- Moon market (local production + trade access)
- Mars market (local production + trade access)
- Earth orbit market (local production + trade access)
- Four Earth faction markets (local + trade access)

**Trade mechanics:**
- Supply in each market = local production + imports from other markets
- Trade links form **dynamically** based on profit margins
- Price determined by: supply/demand + tariffs + logistics costs (distance)
- Example: Moon metal is cheap (abundant mines) → Earth orbit metal is expensive (no mines) → trade route forms Moon→Earth orbit until margin shrinks to unprofitable

---

## Corporation Alignment & Flexibility

- Corporations start with **initial alignment to one Earth faction** (e.g., "US Corp")
- Starting alignment grants bonuses (flavor for early game)
- **Not hard-locked:** Corporations can pivot to trade with other factions if more profitable
- Open questions: Does switching allegiance have political/economic costs? Do aligned factions respond negatively to defection?

---

## Game Tone & Mechanics Direction

**Chosen tone:** Serious, grounded **resource simulation** (not dark capitalism, not Star Trek exploration)

**Implications:**
- Mechanics focus on logistics, supply chains, market competition
- Flavor is analytical, not satirical
- No need for: labor exploitation, bribery, environmental collapse, worker strikes
- Core tension: **Corp vs. Corp economic competition** (who produces cheapest, captures markets first?)

---

## Primary vs. Secondary Mechanics

**Primary loop (turns 1–N):**
- Build infrastructure (mines, factories, refineries) in strategic locations
- Optimize production chains for efficiency
- Compete with other corps through pricing and market dominance

**Secondary/tertiary mechanics (discovered later):**
- Regulation and lobbying (influence restrictions to access new markets)
- Discovery and exploration (new asteroids, resource nodes)
- Diplomacy and bureaucracy (faction relations)

---

## Early Game: Placement & Building

**Early-game primary lever:** Choosing **what to build where**

- Player influence is through infrastructure placement, not political maneuvering
- Factions are passive demand sources (you don't negotiate—you just sell to them)
- Questions deferred: Can you build anywhere? What resources are required to build? Do some locations unlock late-game?

---

## Late Game: Independence & Win Conditions

Multiple possible endgames:
1. **Military dominance:** Build military force, invade/conquer Earth
2. **Colony expansion:** Establish fully self-sufficient off-world colonies (Moon, Mars, etc.)
3. **Exodus:** Build a generation ship to leave the solar system entirely

**Friction with factions increases late-game:**
- If pursuing independence/military, Earth factions may see you as a threat
- Potential for embargo, sanctions, or military response
- Late-game mechanics likely involve faction politics (alliances, leverage, coercion)

---

## Key Design Questions (Still Open)

1. **Population mechanics:** How do workers reach off-world colonies? Incentive-driven (consumer goods) early? Coercion-enabled late?
2. **Progression:** Are late-game actions (military, colonization, coercion) unlocked gradually, or available from turn 1 with escalating consequences?
3. **Faction response:** How do Earth factions respond to corporate threats? Passive/economic only, or can they mount military defense?
4. **Victory conditions:** Is multiple winning paths possible, or designed around one preferred endgame?
5. **Building constraints:** Unlimited building anywhere, or locked to certain locations/techs?

---

## Next Steps

- Solidify **how corporations actually produce and compete** (price wars? Supply control? Vertical integration?)
- Define **early-game progression path** (what unlocks when?)
- Clarify **faction mechanics** (response to late-game threats, diplomatic leverage)
- Design **late-game friction** (what makes independence/invasion costly?)

---

## Notes

- Game mechanics heavily influenced by tone choice (serious → different systems than wacky or exploration-focused)
- Victoria 3's economic model is useful reference for market simulation
- Focus on systems that create interesting **player choice** and **emergent competition**
